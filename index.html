About Me

I am a Commissioning Scientist at the SARAO offices in Cape Town, South Africa.  At SARAO we are developing and operating the revolutionary and science-eager radio telescope called MeerKAT.  You can keep up to date with the latest MeerKAT developments here.  


I have a background in Theoretical Physics, but since my Master's and PhD studies I have become evermore involved with Astrophysics and observational Radio Astronomy. 

My current research interests as part of the Meertime collaboration include pulsars, rapidly neutron stars that emit beamed radio emission. We observe the signals from these blitzing objects as periodic pulses using MeerKAT.  More links to my interests and publications are below. 


I am passionate about the promotion and development of science and specifically Astrophysics in South Africa. The MeerKAT and upcoming SKA project offers wonderful job opportunities for new young lovers of maths and science, that want to study the universe beyond.

My research interests

  • Pulsars and pulsar timing


Pulsars are born in supernova explosions as massive stars end their lives.  The tiny but super dense dead core that stays behind spins rapidly around its axes emitting radio beams from its magnetic poles. These beams are picked up as pulses in our telescopes. The MeerTIME pulsar timing project running on MeerKAT has already eagerly timed more than a 1000 of these radio clocks, smashing records for the precision with which they are timed. Ultimately, we aim to time a network of pulsars (a pulsar timing array) to search for gravitational waves disturbing their clockwork.


  • Fast Radio Bursts


Observations of these often once-off, but sometimes repeating, luminous radio bursts show that they have traveled from distant galaxies to meet our telescopes. The TRAPUM programme on MeerKAT scans the skies regularly for new Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs), and has collected its first one recently. With the overall number of known FRBs currently increasing from month to month, we hope to soon learn a lot more about these mysterious events.


  • The Interstellar Medium


Pulsar signals travel through the Interstellar Medium (ISM) before they reach our telescopes.  Electrons adrift in this medium will impart their signature on pulsar signals by slowing down the radio signal and scattering it along multiple pathways. By studying the effect of the ISM on pulsar signals we get to learn more about the ISM. And are finding  that the ISM has more clumpy structure than previously expected.


  • Bumpy Black Holes


Could we tell from observations if black holes were not as smooth and simple as the standard Kerr model shows? By observing probes like pulsars orbiting super massive black holes (SMBH) closely, we hope to do so. But first we need to catch a pulsar around a SMBH! Fingers crossed that the SKA will find one, perhaps even in the Milky Way.
